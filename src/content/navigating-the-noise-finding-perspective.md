There was a period in 2022 when I seriously questioned whether the Vietnamese renewable energy industry had a future.

Multiple major international developers had announced exits from the market. FIT deadlines had passed, and the new regulatory framework was unclear. Project financing had dried up. Engineers and project managers who had built careers in the sector were updating their CVs, looking for jobs in construction, oil and gas, or different countries altogether.

The noise was deafening. Industry forums were filled with pessimism. News articles painted a picture of an industry in collapse. LinkedIn posts oscillated between despair and denial. Friends in the industry called each other, sharing rumors about which company would exit next.

And then I did something that changed my perspective completely. I stopped scrolling and looked backward.

> In turbulent times, the most valuable thing you can do is zoom out. Not to ignore the problems, but to see them in the context of the trajectory that brought you here.

---

## The View from 2012

Let me take you back to 2012. I was a young engineer in Vietnam, and the renewable energy industry here was essentially non-existent.

There were no utility-scale wind farms. No solar farms. No feed-in tariff. No Power Development Plan with renewable energy targets. No dedicated regulatory framework. No local contractors with wind or solar experience. No training programs. No industry conferences. No jobs.

If you had told someone in 2012 that within 8 years, Vietnam would install over 16 GW of solar and 4 GW of wind, becoming the largest solar market in Southeast Asia and one of the fastest-growing wind markets globally — they would have thought you were delusional.

But it happened. Not smoothly, not perfectly, not without enormous challenges — but it happened.

### The Timeline That Most People Forget

| Year | Milestone | Industry Significance |
|------|----------|---------------------|
| 2011 | Decision 37: Vietnam's first FIT for wind power ($7.8 cents/kWh) | Virtually no response — too low, too uncertain |
| 2017 | Decision 11: Solar FIT introduced ($9.35 cents/kWh) | Triggered the solar boom |
| 2018 | ~100 MW solar installed | First wave of small projects |
| 2019 | Vietnam installs 5 GW of rooftop solar in 12 months | Explosive, chaotic, but transformative |
| 2020 | Decision 39: Onshore wind FIT ($8.5 cents/kWh) | Triggered the wind rush |
| 2021 | 4 GW+ of wind capacity racing for COD before FIT expiry | Construction at unprecedented scale |
| 2022 | FIT expiry, regulatory uncertainty, developer exits | The "crisis" period |
| 2023 | PDP8 approved with 21 GW of onshore wind by 2030 | New policy framework emerges |
| 2024 | New competitive auction mechanisms take shape | Market matures, albeit slowly |

Source: GWEC, EVN, Vietnam MOIT

When you see this timeline, the 2022 "crisis" looks different. Not trivial — the pain was real. But the trajectory is unmistakable: a decade-long transformation from zero to 20+ GW of renewable capacity, with institutional knowledge, local capabilities, and infrastructure that didn't exist before.

## The Pattern: Every Industry Has Cycles

The renewable energy industry's boom-bust-recovery pattern isn't unique. Every growing industry goes through cycles of euphoria, correction, and maturation. Understanding this pattern doesn't eliminate the pain of the down cycle, but it provides essential perspective.

### The Innovation Adoption Cycle Applied to Vietnam RE

| Phase | Period | Characteristics | Emotional State |
|-------|--------|----------------|----------------|
| **Early adoption** | 2011–2017 | Few pioneers, regulatory uncertainty, small projects | Curiosity, skepticism |
| **Growth/Euphoria** | 2018–2021 | Rapid scaling, easy financing, talent inflow | Excitement, FOMO |
| **Correction** | 2022–2023 | Regulatory gaps, exits, financing drought, job losses | Fear, pessimism |
| **Maturation** | 2024–onwards | Clearer regulations, competitive procurement, professionalization | Cautious optimism |

The mistake most people make is projecting the current phase indefinitely. During euphoria, they assume growth will never slow. During correction, they assume the industry is permanently damaged. Both are wrong.

## What the Noise Obscures

### The Structural Case for Renewable Energy Hasn't Changed

When I strip away the noise — the regulatory delays, the political commentary, the LinkedIn hot takes — and look at the fundamental drivers, the picture is clear:

**Vietnam's electricity demand is growing at 8–10% annually.** According to EVN's own projections, Vietnam needs approximately 150 GW of generation capacity by 2030 (up from ~80 GW in 2023). That's 70 GW of new capacity in roughly 7 years.

**Renewable energy is the cheapest source of new generation.** IRENA's Renewable Power Generation Costs 2024 report shows that utility-scale solar PV achieves an LCOE of $35–50/MWh globally, and onshore wind achieves $40–60/MWh — both significantly cheaper than new coal or gas plants, even before considering carbon costs.

**Climate commitments are binding.** Vietnam's commitment to net-zero by 2050 (COP26), the Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP) with $15.5 billion in international financing commitments, and the growing pressure from international trade partners (EU CBAM) all point in the same direction.

**The local capability built over the past decade doesn't disappear.** The contractors who built 4 GW of wind farms didn't forget how. The engineers who designed solar plants didn't lose their skills. The supply chains didn't dissolve. This institutional knowledge is the most valuable asset the industry has — and it persists through market cycles.

### What Actually Changed

The things that actually changed during the "crisis" period were:
1. **Regulatory framework**: The FIT expired without a clear replacement mechanism. This created genuine uncertainty — but uncertainty is different from industry collapse.
2. **Financing conditions**: Global interest rate increases made project finance more expensive. This is cyclical, not structural.
3. **Some international developers exited**: This is painful but also natural. Not every company that enters an emerging market succeeds. The exits created opportunities for better-capitalized and more patient developers.

## How I Navigate the Noise: A Personal Framework

Over the years, I've developed a simple framework for maintaining perspective during turbulent periods. It's not sophisticated — but it works.

### 1. Zoom Out to the 10-Year View

Whatever is happening right now — good or bad — is almost certainly a single chapter in a much longer story. Before reacting to the latest headline, ask: "What will this look like in 10 years?" Most crises look like small bumps on a long upward trajectory. Most booms look like temporary accelerations followed by necessary corrections.

### 2. Separate Signal from Noise

Not all information is equally valuable. Most news coverage — especially in industry media — is noise. It's optimized for clicks and engagement, not for helping you make better decisions.

**Signal**: Changes in fundamental economics (LCOE trends, financing costs, demand projections), regulatory decisions with direct financial impact, technology breakthroughs with proven cost implications.

**Noise**: Executive quotes, industry predictions without data, LinkedIn opinion posts, rumors about company decisions.

Focus on signal. Ignore noise. Your mental clarity will improve dramatically.

### 3. Invest in Yourself During Down Cycles

Down cycles are the best time to build capabilities — precisely because everyone else is retrenching. The engineers who used the 2022–2023 slowdown to get PMP certifications, learn new skills (energy storage, grid integration, AI applications), or build professional networks positioned themselves for the recovery.

When the market turns (and it always does), the people with updated skills and strong networks capture the best opportunities.

### 4. Maintain Your Physical and Mental Foundation

This sounds like wellness advice, but it's genuinely strategic. Turbulent periods are stressful. Stress degrades decision-making. Poor decisions during stressful periods compound the damage.

The counterintuitive response is to invest more in your foundation during tough times, not less:
- Physical health (exercise, sleep, nutrition) sustains your energy and cognitive function
- Relationships (family, friends, colleagues) provide perspective and support
- Financial buffer (savings, reduced spending) provides optionality
- Learning (reading, courses, conversations) maintains forward momentum

### 5. Take the Long View on Career

A career in renewable energy is a 30–40 year proposition. Any individual market cycle — boom or bust — is a 2–5 year period within that span. The professionals who build the most successful careers are the ones who maintain long-term commitment through the cycles, not the ones who jump ship at the first sign of difficulty.

That said, maintaining long-term commitment doesn't mean passive waiting. It means actively building capability, expanding your network, exploring adjacent opportunities, and positioning yourself for the next growth phase.

## What I Wish I Had Known Earlier

Looking back on my career journey — from a young engineer who could barely afford a Honda motorbike to navigating the complexities of renewable energy project management — there are several things I wish someone had told me:

1. **The early years feel slow.** But they're when you build the foundational knowledge and relationships that compound later. Don't skip them.

2. **Industry cycles are features, not bugs.** They create the market conditions that separate committed professionals from opportunists. Be the former.

3. **Your network is your net worth.** Not in a transactional sense — in a genuine, "I've helped these people and they've helped me" sense. The Vietnamese renewable energy community is remarkably small. Everyone knows everyone. Your reputation is your most valuable asset.

4. **Technical skills get you in the door. Perspective keeps you in the room.** The ability to see beyond the current crisis, to connect today's challenges to tomorrow's opportunities, and to communicate that vision clearly — that's what separates managers from leaders.

5. **The best time to plant a tree was 10 years ago. The second best time is today.** Whether it's learning a new skill, building a relationship, or starting a side project — don't wait for perfect conditions. They don't exist.

## The View from Here

As I write this in early 2026, the Vietnamese renewable energy market is showing clear signs of recovery. PDP8 implementation is progressing. Competitive auction mechanisms are taking shape. International capital is returning, albeit more selectively. The Just Energy Transition Partnership is mobilizing its $15.5 billion commitment.

The professionals who maintained their commitment through the difficult period — who continued learning, building relationships, and developing expertise — are now in the strongest position they've ever been. They have the skills, the networks, and the perspective that new entrants lack.

The storm didn't destroy the industry. It tested it. And the industry — along with the people who stayed the course — came through stronger.

---

## The Honest Take

Turbulent times are genuinely difficult. I'm not minimizing the stress of regulatory uncertainty, job insecurity, or financial pressure. These are real challenges that affect real people and real families.

But difficulty and disaster are not the same thing. Difficulty is a feature of growth industries going through maturation. Disaster would be if the fundamental case for renewable energy had collapsed — if the technology had failed, if the economics had reversed, if the climate imperative had disappeared. None of those things happened.

The noise will always be there. The headlines will always oscillate between euphoria and despair. The key is to develop the perspective to see through the noise to the underlying trajectory — and the discipline to keep building, learning, and connecting through every phase of the cycle.

Every storm eventually passes. The question is not whether the sun will come out again — it will. The question is: **what will you have built during the storm?**
