When I first walked the Mekong Delta coastline where a nearshore wind farm was being planned, the most striking thing wasn't the open water or the wind. It was the mud. Soft, deep, seemingly endless alluvial sediment stretching from the riverbank out into the shallow coastal waters.

That mud tells you almost everything you need to know about why foundation selection in Vietnam's nearshore wind sector is so different from Europe, and why the decisions made in the geotechnical assessment phase determine the entire project's technical and commercial viability.

> In nearshore wind, the foundation isn't just a structural element. It's the single largest driver of BOP cost, construction schedule, and technical risk. Getting the foundation choice wrong can kill a project.

According to GWEC's Global Wind Report 2024, Vietnam installed approximately 4.6 GW of wind capacity by end of 2023, making it the largest wind market in Southeast Asia. A significant portion of this capacity — particularly in the southern provinces around Bac Lieu, Soc Trang, and Tra Vinh — consists of nearshore projects in the intertidal and shallow coastal zones of the Mekong Delta. These projects face foundation engineering challenges that are fundamentally different from the deep-water offshore projects in the North Sea or the US East Coast.

This article examines the foundation selection process for nearshore wind farms in Vietnam, using the Mekong Delta nearshore area as a case study — and draws on the practical lessons from projects that have been built in these conditions.

## Foundation Types: The Options on the Table

There are five primary foundation types used in the global wind energy industry:

| Foundation Type | Typical Water Depth | Typical Soil Conditions | Global Market Share (2023) |
|----------------|-------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Monopile** | 10–40m | Sand, clay, mixed | ~77% (dominant in Europe) |
| **Jacket** | 25–60m+ | Variable, often hard soils | ~12% |
| **Gravity-Based Structure (GBS)** | 0–30m | Firm, flat seabed | ~5% |
| **Pile Cap (Pile Group)** | 0–15m | Soft soils, alluvial | ~4% (primarily Asia) |
| **Tripod** | 25–50m | Variable | ~2% |

Source: GWEC Global Wind Report 2024, WindEurope Statistics 2023

The global dominance of monopiles (77% market share) reflects European conditions — moderate water depths, relatively firm North Sea soils, and a mature supply chain of fabrication yards and installation vessels. But this European template doesn't transfer directly to Southeast Asian conditions.

## The Screening Process: Eliminating Options

Foundation selection isn't about finding the "best" option in abstract. It's about systematically eliminating options that don't work for your specific site conditions, then optimizing among the remaining candidates.

### Step 1: Water Depth Screening

For a typical Mekong Delta nearshore project, water depths range from 0 to 15 meters. Some turbine positions are in the intertidal zone — literally exposed at low tide. This immediately narrows the field:

- **Jacket**: Designed for deeper water (25m+). In shallow water, a jacket structure is structurally over-designed, excessively expensive, and presents installation challenges — the jacket footprint becomes disproportionately large relative to the water depth. **Eliminated.**
- **Tripod**: Similarly designed for deeper water (25m+). The three-legged structure provides no advantage in shallow conditions and adds fabrication and installation complexity. **Eliminated.**

Remaining candidates: Monopile, Gravity-Based Structure (GBS), Pile Cap.

### Step 2: Geotechnical Screening

This is where the Mekong Delta's geology becomes the decisive factor. Extensive geotechnical investigations — including borehole drilling and cone penetration testing (CPT) — have been conducted across multiple project sites in the region. The results consistently show:

**Typical Mekong Delta soil profile:**

| Depth Below Seabed | Soil Type | Condition | SPT N-Value |
|-------------------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| 0–5m | Very soft clay / silt | Near-liquid, highly compressible | 0–2 |
| 5–12m | Soft to firm clay | Slightly more competent, still compressible | 2–8 |
| 12–20m | Stiff clay with sand lenses | Variable, occasional thin sand layers | 8–20 |
| 20–30m | Dense sand / stiff clay | Competent bearing stratum | 20–40 |
| 30m+ | Very dense sand / hard clay | Foundation bearing layer | 40+ |

The critical observation: **the top 15–20 meters of soil are extremely soft.** SPT N-values of 0–8 in the upper layers mean the soil has very low shear strength and high compressibility. This has profound implications for foundation selection.

- **Gravity-Based Structure (GBS)**: Requires a firm, flat seabed to transfer structural loads through direct bearing. With 15–20m of soft clay, a GBS would experience excessive settlement and potential bearing capacity failure. GBS foundations in similar conditions would require massive ground improvement (stone columns, preloading) that would be prohibitively expensive. **Eliminated.**

Remaining candidates: **Monopile** and **Pile Cap (Pile Group)**.

### Step 3: Constructability and Installation Assessment

Both monopile and pile cap foundations can technically work in soft soils — they both transfer loads through pile friction and end-bearing in the deeper competent strata. But constructability is where they diverge dramatically for Mekong Delta conditions.

## Monopile vs. Pile Cap: The Deep Dive

### Monopile in Shallow Water: The Challenges

A monopile is a single large-diameter steel tube (typically 6–10m diameter for modern turbines) driven into the seabed using a hydraulic impact hammer or vibratory hammer. It's the workhorse of the European offshore wind industry for good reason — it's simple, proven, and efficient in the right conditions.

But in the Mekong Delta nearshore context, monopiles face several significant challenges:

**1. Installation vessel requirements**

Monopile installation requires a jack-up vessel or heavy-lift vessel with:
- A crane capacity of 800–2,000 tonnes (for lifting and upending the monopile)
- A hydraulic hammer capable of driving piles through 20+ meters of soft soil to reach the bearing stratum
- Jack-up legs rated for the specific soil conditions (leg penetration in soft clay is unpredictable)

In water depths of 3–10m, mobilizing these large vessels to the installation location is extremely difficult. The approach channels are often shallow, silted, and affected by tidal variations. Jack-up vessels designed for 25m+ water depths may not even be able to reach shallow nearshore sites.

**2. Supply chain constraints**

Monopile fabrication requires specialized steel rolling and welding facilities capable of producing cylinders 6–10m in diameter with wall thicknesses of 60–100mm. As of 2024, there are limited fabrication facilities in Southeast Asia with this capability. Most monopiles for the Asian market are manufactured in China, South Korea, or Europe, requiring:
- Long procurement lead times (12–18 months)
- Expensive marine transportation to the project site
- Heavy-lift port facilities for offloading

**3. Cost implications**

Based on industry benchmarks, monopile foundation costs (including transition piece) for a shallow-water Asian project typically range from **$2.5–4.0M per turbine position** — significantly higher than in European projects due to supply chain logistics and vessel mobilization costs.

| Monopile Cost Component | Estimated Range |
|------------------------|----------------|
| Steel fabrication & coating | $1.2–2.0M |
| Transportation to site | $300–600K |
| Installation (vessel, hammer, grouting) | $800K–1.2M |
| Transition piece | $400–600K |
| **Total per position** | **$2.5–4.0M** |

### Pile Cap (Pile Group): The Advantages

A pile cap foundation consists of a thick reinforced concrete platform (the "cap") supported by a group of smaller-diameter steel or concrete piles (typically 800mm–1,500mm diameter). The piles are driven through the soft upper soils to reach the competent bearing stratum, and the concrete cap distributes the turbine loads across the entire pile group.

For Mekong Delta nearshore projects, pile cap foundations offer decisive advantages:

**1. Installation logistics**

Pile cap construction uses smaller, shallower-draft equipment:
- Smaller piles (800–1,500mm vs. 6,000–10,000mm) can be transported on standard barges
- Pile driving uses smaller hammers that can be mounted on barge-based crawler cranes
- Concrete work uses standard marine concrete batching and pumping equipment
- The entire operation can work in water depths as shallow as 2–3 meters

This is critical in the Mekong Delta, where approach channels are shallow, silted, and subject to tidal restrictions. Barges drawing 2–3m can reach sites that would be inaccessible to large jack-up vessels.

**2. Local manufacturing capability**

This is perhaps the most significant advantage. Pile cap construction uses materials and techniques that are well-established in Vietnam's construction industry:
- **Steel piles**: Standard steel pipe piles (800–1,500mm diameter) are manufactured by multiple Vietnamese steel mills
- **Concrete**: Reinforced concrete construction is a core competency of local marine contractors
- **Rebar and formwork**: Standard construction materials available domestically

According to Vietnam's localization requirements for wind projects, using locally manufactured components significantly reduces import duties and logistics costs. Pile cap foundations typically achieve 60–80% local content, compared to 10–20% for imported monopiles.

**3. Local contractor experience**

Vietnam has decades of experience in marine pile group construction — from bridge foundations to port infrastructure to building foundations in the alluvial Mekong Delta region. Contractors understand the soil conditions, have the right equipment fleet, and have skilled crews experienced in working with soft clays.

By contrast, monopile installation is a specialized discipline with a limited pool of experienced contractors in Southeast Asia. The skills required — operating large hydraulic hammers, managing pile driving in variable soils, grouting transition piece connections — are not widely available locally.

**4. Cost comparison**

| Pile Cap Cost Component | Estimated Range |
|------------------------|----------------|
| Steel piles (8–12 per foundation, locally manufactured) | $400–800K |
| Pile driving (barge + crane + hammer) | $300–600K |
| Concrete pile cap (casting, rebar, formwork) | $300–500K |
| Transportation via Mekong Delta river network | $50–150K |
| **Total per position** | **$1.2–2.5M** |

The cost advantage is substantial: pile cap foundations are typically **40–50% cheaper** than monopile foundations for Mekong Delta nearshore projects, primarily due to local manufacturing, simpler logistics, and lower vessel costs.

## The Engineering Behind the Pile Cap

For those interested in the technical details, here's how pile cap foundations work in the Mekong Delta:

### Load Transfer Mechanism

The wind turbine generates several types of loads that the foundation must resist:
- **Vertical load**: Self-weight of the turbine, tower, and nacelle (~3,000–5,000 kN for a 4–6 MW turbine)
- **Horizontal load**: Wind and wave forces acting on the rotor and tower (~1,000–3,000 kN at seabed level)
- **Overturning moment**: The dominant load case — wind force on the rotor creates a large moment at the foundation (~50,000–150,000 kNm)

The pile group resists these loads through:
1. **Axial capacity**: Piles develop friction along their length in the surrounding soil (skin friction) and bearing capacity at their tips (end bearing)
2. **Lateral capacity**: Piles resist horizontal forces through passive soil pressure along their embedded length
3. **Moment resistance**: The pile group arrangement — typically 8–12 piles arranged in a circular or rectangular pattern — resists overturning through the push-pull couple of compression and tension piles

### Design Considerations

| Parameter | Typical Range (Mekong Delta) |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Number of piles per foundation | 8–12 |
| Pile diameter | 800–1,500mm |
| Pile length (below seabed) | 25–45m |
| Pile cap diameter/width | 12–18m |
| Pile cap thickness | 2.0–3.5m |
| Concrete grade | C40–C50 (marine grade) |
| Design life | 25–30 years |
| Scour protection | Rock armour or concrete mattress |

### Construction Sequence

A typical pile cap foundation is built in the following sequence:

1. **Seabed preparation**: Dredging or leveling the seabed at the foundation location
2. **Template installation**: A steel frame (template) is lowered to the seabed to guide pile placement
3. **Pile driving**: Each pile is driven through the template to the design penetration depth using a hydraulic or diesel hammer
4. **Template removal**: The guiding template is removed after all piles are in position
5. **Formwork installation**: Steel or GRP formwork panels are installed around the pile group
6. **Rebar installation**: Reinforcement cages are placed within the formwork
7. **Concrete pouring**: Marine-grade concrete is poured to form the pile cap, typically in a single continuous pour
8. **Curing**: The concrete cures for 28 days to achieve design strength
9. **Tower base installation**: The tower flange or anchor cage is embedded in the pile cap during concrete pouring

Total duration per foundation: typically **4–8 weeks** including mobilization and demobilization.

## Global Context: Why This Matters Beyond Vietnam

The Mekong Delta case study illustrates a broader principle in offshore wind: **foundation selection is site-specific.** The European template of monopile dominance doesn't apply universally.

| Region | Dominant Foundation | Reason |
|--------|-------------------|--------|
| North Sea (Europe) | Monopile (80%+) | Moderate depths, firm sands, mature supply chain |
| Taiwan | Jacket (60%+) | Deep water (20–50m), seismic loads, typhoon conditions |
| Japan | Floating / Jacket | Very deep water, seismic requirements |
| Mekong Delta (Vietnam) | Pile Cap (90%+) | Shallow water, very soft soils, local manufacturing |
| China (nearshore) | Pile Cap / GBS | Shallow water, soft soils, massive local supply chain |

As the global offshore wind industry expands into new markets — India, the Philippines, Brazil, Australia — each region will need to develop foundation solutions optimized for local conditions, supply chains, and contractor capabilities. The European monopile playbook is a starting point, not a universal answer.

## Lessons Learned from the Field

### Lesson 1: Don't Underestimate Pile Driving in Soft Clay

In very soft clay, piles can "free-fall" during driving — sinking under their own weight without hammer blows. This sounds convenient but actually creates problems:
- Pile alignment is harder to control during free-fall
- The pile may penetrate deeper than intended, affecting the pile cap elevation
- Neighboring piles can be disturbed by soil displacement from adjacent driving

**Mitigation**: Use a driving guide frame throughout the installation, monitor pile penetration in real-time, and have contingency plans for over-penetration.

### Lesson 2: Concrete Work in Marine Environments Is Demanding

Pouring 200–400 cubic meters of concrete for a pile cap in an intertidal zone requires careful planning:
- Concrete must be delivered by barge and pumped to the formwork — any delay in delivery can cause cold joints
- Tidal windows restrict working hours — you may only have 6–8 hours per tidal cycle for concrete operations
- Marine concrete mix design must resist chloride penetration and satisfy durability requirements over a 25–30 year design life

**Mitigation**: Plan for continuous pours, use retarding admixtures to extend workability, and test concrete mix designs extensively before production.

### Lesson 3: Scour Protection Is Not Optional

In tidal and current-prone environments, scour (erosion of the seabed around foundations) can undermine pile capacity if not addressed. In the Mekong Delta, with its strong tidal currents and fine-grained seabed sediments, scour depths of 1.0–2.5 meters have been observed around pile group foundations.

**Mitigation**: Design scour protection (rock armour or concrete mattresses) as part of the foundation scope, not as an afterthought. Monitor scour development during operation using periodic bathymetric surveys.

## The Honest Take

Foundation selection for nearshore wind in Vietnam isn't a mystery — the engineering logic consistently points toward pile cap foundations for the Mekong Delta and similar nearshore environments. The combination of very soft alluvial soils, shallow water depths, limited large-vessel access, strong local manufacturing capability, and experienced local contractors makes the pile cap the clear winner on almost every dimension: technical feasibility, cost, schedule, and risk.

That said, the pile cap isn't a universal solution. For future Vietnamese offshore projects in deeper water (15m+) with firmer seabed conditions, monopile or jacket foundations may become competitive — especially as the regional fabrication supply chain develops and installation vessel availability improves.

The most important lesson from the Mekong Delta experience is this: **foundation selection must be driven by site-specific data, not by global market conventions.** What works in the North Sea doesn't necessarily work in the South China Sea. The projects that succeed are the ones that start from the soil data and work upward, not from a preferred foundation type and hope it fits.
