# Nearshore Wind Farm Foundations in Vietnam — The Case Study in Mekong Delta Nearshore Area

*By techmadeeasy team*
*Editor: Trang Tran*

There are several types of foundation available for nearshore/offshore wind towers. You may have heard of the names before: gravity-based structure (GBS), monopile, pile cap, jacket, and tripod. The choice of foundation is one of the key determining factors in both technical and commercial feasibility studies of a nearshore/offshore wind farm project.

## Key Selection Factors

The choice depends on several critical factors:

- Water depth and met-ocean conditions
- Wind turbine class and frequency range
- Geotechnical survey results
- Constructability and installation technology of the contractor
- Material supply and local manufacturing capability
- Construction timeline
- Extreme natural conditions (storms, earthquakes)

---

## Eliminating Options: The Screening Process

### Step 1: Water Depth

Generally for a nearshore wind farm in very shallow water (depth between 3m to 10m), we can immediately eliminate the **jacket** and **tripod** foundation types as they are simply more suitable for greater water depth projects.

### Step 2: Geotechnical Survey

Next, geotechnical survey is brought in to determine soil conditions below the seabed. Multiple borehole tests are drilled into the seabed to determine how "sturdy" the conditions below are. For instance, the tests done on the Mekong Delta area Nearshore Wind project showed that the soil components below seabed consist of multiple layers of different conditions. Approximately 20m of the top layer is very soft cohesive soil, turning to clay/sand with stiff, low SPT (Standard Penetration Test) values.

Therefore, for such soil conditions, the **Gravity-based structure (GBS)** is not the right type of foundation as GBS would be best used when we have a firm and flat seabed.

### Step 3: Feasibility Assessment

For a typical nearshore project in the Mekong Delta River, the feasible choices left are:

- **Pile cap** (pile group foundation)
- **Monopile**

Both options are ideal candidates to bear the full load of a wind turbine either in idle or operational state on this specific seabed condition.

---

## Monopile vs. Pile Cap: The Final Decision

However, let's see how construction feasibility and installation ability tests would help us further screen out another type of foundation.

### Monopile Challenges

Monopile piling work would be quite challenging for nearshore, shallow-depth projects as it would require **large jack-up vessels** to drive those heavy piles into the seabed. Mobilizing these heavy vessels plus equipment to the piling location in certain shallow water locations is very difficult, if not impossible.

Moreover, monopiles have to be pre-manufactured in offshore factories and thus require long lead times for transportation to the project location.

### Pile Cap Advantages

The pile cap option offers a better and more feasible solution for nearshore shallow water projects:

- The pile cap is a thick concrete mat-like structure which rests on a group of smaller piles, providing a stable foundation
- Smaller piles are easier to mobilize to the project location using barges
- Barges are far better suited as shallow water transportation vessels
- Pile group and pile cap can be **manufactured locally**, requiring less time to transport to project sites via the Mekong Delta River network and/or the East Sea

### Local Contractor Experience

A final consideration is the skillset and experience of local contractors. Whilst local contractors have limited exposure and manufacturing experience with monopiles, there are numerous contractors with track records in construction of pile cap structures.

---

## Conclusion

As you can see, there are a number of reasons why the **pile cap structure** is the usual foundation of choice for a nearshore project, especially those around the Mekong River Delta region. It is certainly not the only solution but generally is a better one in terms of feasibility.
