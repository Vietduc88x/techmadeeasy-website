When we ran our first Renewable Energy Workshop, the most common question from participants wasn't about solar panel efficiency or wind turbine specifications. It was: **"Where do I even start?"**

That question — simple, honest, and universal — is what this workshop was designed to answer. Whether you're a fresh graduate exploring career options, an engineer transitioning from oil and gas, or a project manager looking to understand the renewable energy landscape, the starting point is the same: understanding the fundamentals, knowing the numbers, and seeing where the opportunities are.

> The renewable energy transition is the largest infrastructure investment cycle in human history. Understanding it isn't optional — it's career-critical. But the amount of information available can be overwhelming. This workshop cuts through the noise.

According to IRENA's World Energy Transitions Outlook 2024, achieving the 1.5°C Paris Agreement target requires tripling global renewable energy capacity to 11,000 GW by 2030 — from approximately 3,900 GW at the end of 2023. That's 7,100 GW of new capacity in roughly 6 years. The investment required: $35 trillion in renewable energy generation alone by 2030.

These numbers aren't abstract projections. They represent real projects that need real engineers, project managers, financiers, lawyers, and operators. Understanding the fundamentals is the first step to participating in this transformation.

## What the Workshop Covers

### The Energy Transition in Numbers

The scale of the global energy transition is difficult to grasp without data. Here are the numbers that frame everything else:

| Metric | 2015 | 2023 | 2030 Target | Source |
|--------|------|------|-------------|--------|
| Global renewable capacity | 1,850 GW | 3,900 GW | 11,000 GW | IRENA |
| Annual renewable investment | $285B | $500B+ | $1.3T+ needed | BloombergNEF |
| Solar PV LCOE (global average) | $100/MWh | $49/MWh | $30–40/MWh expected | IRENA |
| Onshore wind LCOE | $65/MWh | $52/MWh | $40–50/MWh expected | IRENA |
| Battery storage cost (Li-ion) | $380/kWh | $139/kWh | $80–100/kWh expected | BloombergNEF |
| RE share of global electricity | 23% | 30% | 60%+ needed | IEA |

The single most important trend: **cost reduction.** Solar PV costs have declined 90% since 2010. Onshore wind has declined 70%. Battery storage has declined 90% since 2013. These are not marginal improvements — they are fundamental transformations that have made renewable energy the cheapest source of new electricity generation in most of the world.

### Technology Deep Dive

#### Solar Photovoltaic (PV)

Solar PV converts sunlight directly into electricity using semiconductor materials. It's the fastest-growing energy technology in history.

**Key metrics for utility-scale solar (2024):**
- Module efficiency: 21–23% (mono-PERC), 25–27% (TOPCon/HJT)
- System LCOE: $30–50/MWh (region-dependent)
- Capacity factor: 15–25% (latitude-dependent)
- Project size: 50–1,000+ MW per site
- Construction timeline: 12–18 months for utility-scale

**Emerging technology**: Bifacial modules capture reflected light from the ground surface, increasing energy yield by 5–15%. Perovskite-tandem cells in laboratory testing have achieved >33% efficiency, potentially enabling the next wave of cost reduction.

#### Onshore Wind

Onshore wind uses turbine rotors to convert wind kinetic energy into electricity. Mature technology with a proven track record across diverse conditions.

**Key metrics (2024):**
- Turbine capacity: 4–7 MW (onshore standard)
- Rotor diameter: 140–175m
- Hub height: 100–140m
- System LCOE: $40–60/MWh
- Capacity factor: 25–45% (site-dependent)
- Construction timeline: 18–30 months

#### Offshore Wind

Offshore wind captures stronger, more consistent winds over the ocean surface. Higher capacity factors but significantly higher costs due to marine construction.

**Key metrics (2024):**
- Turbine capacity: 12–16 MW (latest generation)
- Rotor diameter: 220–260m
- System LCOE: $75–120/MWh (declining rapidly)
- Capacity factor: 40–55%
- Construction timeline: 3–5 years (including development)

#### Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS)

BESS is the technology that makes variable renewable energy dispatchable — storing excess generation for use when the sun isn't shining or the wind isn't blowing.

**Key metrics (2024):**
- Dominant technology: Lithium-ion (LFP chemistry)
- Duration: 2–4 hours (grid-scale standard)
- System cost: $200–350/kWh installed (4-hour duration)
- Round-trip efficiency: 85–92%
- Cycle life: 5,000–8,000 cycles
- Declining costs: 45% reduction since 2020

For a deeper analysis of BESS, see our article: [BESS: The Silent Revolution Powering the Grid](/blog/bess-silent-revolution-powering-grid)

## The Vietnam Context

Vietnam's renewable energy sector has undergone a remarkable transformation over the past decade:

| Technology | Installed Capacity (2023) | PDP8 Target (2030) | Growth Required |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Solar PV | ~17 GW | ~12.8 GW (revised down) | Existing capacity exceeds target |
| Onshore wind | ~4 GW | ~21.9 GW | ~18 GW additional |
| Offshore wind | ~0.1 GW | ~6 GW | ~5.9 GW additional |
| BESS | Minimal | Significant growth planned | New sector |

Source: PDP8 (Decision 500/QD-TTg), GWEC Vietnam

**Key takeaway**: Vietnam's Power Development Plan 8 (PDP8) positions wind energy — particularly onshore and offshore — as the primary growth area through 2030. The solar sector, which dominated 2019–2021, is relatively saturated in terms of grid capacity. The next wave of development is wind-dominated, with energy storage playing an increasingly critical supporting role.

### Career Implications for Vietnam

The PDP8 targets imply:
- **~18 GW of new onshore wind**: Requiring project developers, construction managers, O&M engineers, and turbine specialists
- **~6 GW of new offshore wind**: A completely new discipline for Vietnam, requiring marine engineers, offshore construction specialists, and specialized legal/finance professionals
- **Grid modernization**: Smart grid technology, SCADA systems, and grid integration engineering
- **Energy storage**: Battery system design, integration, and operations — a nascent field with enormous growth potential

## Five Things I Wish I Had Known Before My First RE Project

### 1. The Technology Is the Easy Part

Solar panels, wind turbines, and batteries are well-engineered, reliable, and increasingly commoditized. The hard parts are everything around the technology: permitting, land acquisition, grid connection, community engagement, financing, and construction logistics. If you're entering the industry, develop skills in these areas — they're where projects succeed or fail.

### 2. Data Literacy Is Non-Negotiable

Renewable energy is a data-intensive industry. Wind resource assessment, solar irradiation analysis, energy yield forecasting, financial modeling, and grid integration studies all require comfort with data analysis, statistical methods, and modeling tools. Invest in Excel, Python, or specialized tools like PVsyst (solar) or WindPRO (wind) early in your career.

### 3. Regulatory Knowledge Is a Competitive Advantage

The difference between a good renewable energy professional and a great one is often regulatory knowledge. Understanding FIT mechanisms, PPA structures, grid codes, environmental permitting requirements, and local government processes is invaluable — and rare.

### 4. Relationships Matter More Than You Think

The renewable energy community in Southeast Asia is remarkably tight-knit. Your reputation follows you across projects and companies. Invest in genuine relationships with colleagues, contractors, regulators, and community stakeholders. These relationships will be your most valuable professional asset over a 20–30 year career.

### 5. The Industry Rewards Long-Term Thinking

Renewable energy projects have 25–30 year operating lives. The industry rewards professionals who think in decades, not quarters. Build deep expertise, develop a long-term perspective, and resist the temptation to chase short-term trends.
