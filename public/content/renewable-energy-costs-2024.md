# Renewable Power Generation Cost in 2024: Source IRENA

![Renewable Energy Costs](/images/solar_farm.png)
*Solar PV — now the cheapest source of new electricity in most of the world, with costs down 90% since 2010*

The numbers don't lie. Renewable energy has crossed the tipping point — and the cost data from IRENA's Renewable Power Generation Costs 2024 report makes the case definitively.

This article breaks down the latest cost trends across all major renewable technologies, explains what's driving the decline, and explores what it means for developers, investors, and project managers working in the energy transition.

> "In 2023, 86% of all newly commissioned renewable power generation capacity had costs lower than newly commissioned fossil fuel-fired capacity." — IRENA, 2024

## The Big Picture: 14 Years of Cost Revolution

The period from 2010 to 2023 represents the most dramatic cost reduction in the history of energy technology. Here's the summary:

| Technology | LCOE 2010 ($/MWh) | LCOE 2023 ($/MWh) | Cost Reduction | Status |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|--------|
| Solar PV | 360 | 49 | -90% | Cheapest new electricity globally |
| Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) | 340 | 118 | -65% | Niche but declining |
| Onshore Wind | 180 | 52 | -70% | Mature, still improving |
| Offshore Wind | 220 | 75–120 | -55% | Scaling rapidly |
| Battery Storage (Li-ion) | 1,200/kWh (2013) | 139/kWh | -88% | Game-changer for dispatchability |

These aren't incremental improvements. They represent a fundamental restructuring of global energy economics.

## Solar PV: The Unstoppable Force

Solar PV has achieved something remarkable: a 90% cost reduction in 14 years, making it the cheapest source of new electricity generation in most of the world.

### Key Cost Drivers

**Module costs** have been the primary driver, declining from over $4/W in 2010 to under $0.20/W for utility-scale mono-PERC modules in 2023. This is driven by:

- Manufacturing scale: Global annual module production exceeded 450 GW in 2023
- Technology improvements: Cell efficiency has increased from ~15% to 22-23% (mono-PERC), with TOPCon and HJT cells achieving 25-27%
- Supply chain maturity: Polysilicon, wafer, cell, and module costs have all declined through economies of scale

**Balance of System (BOS) costs** — everything except the modules — have also declined significantly:

- Inverter costs: Down 75% since 2010
- Mounting structures: Down 50% through standardization
- Installation labor: Improved through experience and standardized designs
- Soft costs (permitting, development): Declining more slowly but still trending down

### Regional LCOE Ranges (2023)

| Region | LCOE Range ($/MWh) | Key Factor |
|--------|--------------------|-----------|
| India | 30–40 | Low labor, high irradiation |
| China | 30–45 | Manufacturing dominance |
| Middle East & North Africa | 25–35 | Exceptional solar resource |
| Australia | 35–50 | High irradiation, rising labor costs |
| United States | 40–60 | Higher soft costs, IRA incentives |
| Europe | 45–70 | Lower irradiation, higher labor |
| Southeast Asia | 40–55 | Growing market, improving supply chains |

### The Emerging Technologies

**Bifacial modules** capture reflected light from the ground surface, increasing energy yield by 5–15% with minimal additional cost. Adoption is accelerating rapidly — over 40% of new installations in 2023 used bifacial modules.

**Perovskite-tandem cells** have achieved >33% efficiency in laboratory testing, compared to the ~27% theoretical limit for single-junction silicon. If manufacturing challenges are solved, this technology could drive another 30–50% yield increase on the same footprint, potentially pushing utility-scale LCOE below $20/MWh.

## Onshore Wind: Mature but Still Improving

Onshore wind has achieved a 70% cost reduction since 2010, driven primarily by turbine scaling and improved capacity factors.

### The Scaling Story

| Metric | 2010 | 2023 | Change |
|--------|------|------|--------|
| Average turbine capacity | 1.8 MW | 5.0 MW | +178% |
| Rotor diameter | 80m | 155m | +94% |
| Hub height | 70m | 110m | +57% |
| Capacity factor (global avg) | 27% | 36% | +33% |

Larger rotors sweep more area, capturing more energy. Higher hub heights access stronger, more consistent winds. Together, these improvements have dramatically increased the annual energy production per turbine.

### Cost Breakdown (2023 average)

- **Turbine supply**: $800–1,000/kW (55–60% of total CAPEX)
- **Balance of Plant**: $300–500/kW (25–30%)
- **Development & soft costs**: $150–250/kW (10–15%)
- **Total installed cost**: $1,300–1,800/kW

### Current Challenges

Despite the cost reductions, onshore wind faces headwinds:

- **Supply chain inflation**: Steel, copper, and rare earth prices have increased post-2020
- **Permitting bottlenecks**: Average permitting timelines have increased in Europe and parts of Asia
- **Grid connection delays**: Interconnection queues are growing globally
- **Land availability**: Best sites in mature markets are increasingly developed

## Offshore Wind: The Next Frontier

Offshore wind has achieved a 55% cost reduction since 2010, but the trajectory has been less linear than solar or onshore wind due to the complexity of marine construction.

### The Turbine Revolution

The most dramatic change in offshore wind has been turbine scaling:

| Year | Largest Available Turbine | Rotor Diameter |
|------|--------------------------|----------------|
| 2010 | 3.6 MW (Siemens) | 107m |
| 2015 | 8 MW (Vestas V164) | 164m |
| 2020 | 12 MW (GE Haliade-X) | 220m |
| 2023 | 15 MW (Vestas V236) | 236m |
| 2025+ | 16–18 MW (announced) | 260m+ |

Each doubling of turbine capacity reduces the number of foundations, cables, and installation campaigns required — driving significant cost reduction.

### LCOE Trajectory

- **2010**: $220/MWh — offshore wind was expensive and niche
- **2015**: $150/MWh — first large-scale cost reductions
- **2019**: $80/MWh — record-low auction prices in Europe
- **2023**: $75–120/MWh — wide range reflecting project maturity and location
- **2030 target**: $50–70/MWh — if current trends continue

### Fixed-Bottom vs Floating

**Fixed-bottom** foundations (monopiles, jackets, gravity-based) are proven technology in water depths up to ~60m. They represent 95%+ of current installed offshore wind capacity.

**Floating** foundations (semi-submersible, spar, tension-leg) unlock wind resources in deeper waters — representing approximately 80% of global offshore wind potential. Current cost: $150–200/MWh, but expected to decline rapidly as the industry scales from pilot projects to commercial arrays.

## Battery Energy Storage: The Enabler

Battery storage has achieved the most dramatic cost reduction of any energy technology: 88% since 2013.

### Cost Trajectory

| Year | Li-ion Pack Cost ($/kWh) | Key Milestone |
|------|--------------------------|---------------|
| 2013 | 1,200 | Early utility-scale pilots |
| 2015 | 580 | First commercial BESS projects |
| 2018 | 280 | Cost parity approaching for peaking |
| 2020 | 250 | COVID supply chain disruptions |
| 2022 | 180 | Lithium price spike |
| 2023 | 139 | LFP chemistry dominance |
| 2025E | 100–110 | Expected continued decline |
| 2028E | 80–90 | Target for widespread dispatchability |

### The LFP Revolution

Lithium Iron Phosphate (LFP) chemistry has become the dominant choice for grid-scale storage:

- **Lower cost**: No cobalt or nickel required
- **Longer cycle life**: 5,000–8,000 cycles vs 2,000–3,000 for NMC
- **Better safety**: More thermally stable, reduced fire risk
- **Longer duration**: Enabling 4-hour standard configurations

### System-Level Costs (2023)

For a 100 MW / 400 MWh BESS installation:

- **Battery modules**: $90–120/kWh
- **Power conversion system**: $30–50/kWh
- **Balance of plant**: $20–40/kWh
- **EPC & soft costs**: $30–50/kWh
- **Total installed**: $200–350/kWh

## The Convergence: Solar + Storage

The most transformative development in energy economics is the convergence of solar PV and battery storage. Together, they offer **dispatchable clean energy** — electricity that can be generated, stored, and delivered when needed.

### Combined System Economics (2023)

A typical solar + 4-hour BESS system:

- **Solar LCOE**: $40–50/MWh
- **Storage adder**: $30–50/MWh (levelized)
- **Combined LCOE**: $70–100/MWh

This is already competitive with new gas peaking plants ($90–130/MWh) in many markets. By 2028, combined solar + storage LCOE is expected to drop below $60/MWh in optimal locations.

### The Implications

For project developers and investors:

1. **Solar + BESS is the default**: New renewable projects increasingly include storage as standard
2. **Revenue stacking**: Storage enables energy arbitrage, frequency regulation, and capacity payments
3. **Grid services**: BESS provides ancillary services that solar alone cannot
4. **Reduced curtailment**: Storage absorbs excess generation that would otherwise be curtailed

## What This Means for the Energy Transition

### The Scale of Investment Required

According to IRENA's World Energy Transitions Outlook 2024:

- **Current global RE capacity**: ~3,900 GW (end of 2023)
- **2030 target** (1.5°C pathway): 11,000 GW
- **Gap**: 7,100 GW of new capacity in ~6 years
- **Annual investment needed**: $1.3 trillion+ in renewable generation alone

### The Human Capital Challenge

These numbers translate directly into workforce needs:

- **Project developers**: Site identification, permitting, stakeholder engagement
- **Engineers**: Electrical, civil, mechanical, marine (for offshore)
- **Project managers**: Coordination, risk management, cost control
- **Finance professionals**: Project finance, PPA structuring, insurance
- **O&M specialists**: 25–30 year operating life per project

The renewable energy industry needs hundreds of thousands of skilled professionals over the next decade. Understanding cost dynamics is the foundation for participating in this transformation.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Solar PV is the cheapest**: At $49/MWh globally (and much lower in optimal markets), solar is the default choice for new electricity generation
2. **Battery storage is the enabler**: Without cost-competitive storage, variable renewables can't replace dispatchable fossil fuels. At $139/kWh and falling, that threshold is being crossed
3. **Offshore wind is scaling**: 15+ MW turbines are transforming project economics. Fixed-bottom costs are approaching competitiveness; floating is the next frontier
4. **Convergence changes everything**: Solar + storage is a dispatchable system that competes directly with gas peaking plants
5. **The investment opportunity is massive**: $1.3 trillion+ annually in renewables requires an entire ecosystem of professionals, technologies, and financial instruments

The cost revolution in renewable energy isn't a forecast — it's a fact. The question for professionals, investors, and policymakers is no longer "if" but "how fast" — and whether they're positioned to participate.
