There's a moment in every onshore wind project where the engineering team's elegant design meets the physical reality of Vietnam's road network. It usually happens during the transportation route survey, when someone measures a village road corner and realizes that a 70-meter blade on a 30-meter extendable trailer simply cannot make the turn.

That moment — where a multi-hundred-million-dollar project's schedule depends on whether you can negotiate a 15-meter road widening through a rice paddy — captures everything about onshore wind turbine transportation. It's not a glamorous topic. It doesn't appear in investor presentations. But it is consistently one of the most underestimated risk areas in Vietnamese wind projects.

> Wind turbine transportation is not a logistics problem. It's a planning, permitting, and stakeholder management challenge that happens to involve very large objects on very small roads.

According to GWEC's Global Wind Report 2024, Vietnam's installed wind capacity reached approximately 4.6 GW by end of 2023. The majority of this capacity is onshore — spread across the Central Highlands (Gia Lai, Dak Lak, Dak Nong), the south-central coast (Ninh Thuan, Binh Thuan), and the Mekong Delta provinces. Almost all of these projects faced significant transportation challenges during construction.

This article shares the practical lessons from managing wind turbine transportation in Vietnam — the challenges that catch projects off guard and the planning approaches that prevent them.

## The Scale of the Challenge

Modern wind turbines are enormous. And they're getting larger every year as the industry pushes for higher capacity factors and lower LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy). Understanding the dimensions involved is the first step in appreciating why transportation is so challenging.

### Component Dimensions and Weights

| Component | Typical Dimensions (4–6 MW onshore) | Weight | Transport Challenge |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Blade** | 65–80m length, 4–5m max chord width | 18–25 tonnes | Extreme length, requires specialized trailers |
| **Tower Section 1** (base) | 4.5–5.0m diameter, 20–30m length | 80–120 tonnes | Heavy, wide — road bridges and overpasses |
| **Tower Section 2** (mid) | 4.0–4.5m diameter, 20–25m length | 50–80 tonnes | Wide diameter, weight restrictions |
| **Tower Section 3** (top) | 3.5–4.0m diameter, 15–20m length | 30–50 tonnes | Least challenging tower section |
| **Nacelle** | 12–14m length, 4–5m width, 4–5m height | 100–200 tonnes | Heavy, requires high-capacity trailer |
| **Hub** | 4–5m diameter | 30–50 tonnes | Compact but heavy |

The blade is the most challenging component to transport. Not because of weight — blades are relatively light — but because of their extreme length. A 75-meter blade on a transport trailer creates a swept path of 30+ meters on turns. In Vietnam, where provincial and district roads were designed for motorbikes and small trucks, this creates extraordinary geometric challenges.

### Blade Length Evolution

| Year | Typical Onshore Blade Length | Rotor Diameter | Turbine Capacity |
|------|---------------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| 2010 | 40–45m | 80–90m | 1.5–2.0 MW |
| 2015 | 50–55m | 100–115m | 2.0–3.0 MW |
| 2020 | 60–70m | 130–150m | 3.5–5.0 MW |
| 2024 | 70–85m | 150–175m | 5.0–7.0 MW |

Source: GWEC, BloombergNEF

As turbines scale up, the transportation challenge grows non-linearly. Every additional 10 meters of blade length doesn't just require a slightly longer trailer — it fundamentally changes which road sections are passable and which require modification.

## The Three Core Challenges

### Challenge 1: Road Infrastructure

In Vietnam, wind farm sites are predominantly in rural areas — mountain ridges in the Central Highlands, coastal plains in Ninh Thuan and Binh Thuan, or agricultural flatlands in the Mekong Delta. The roads connecting these sites to the nearest deep-water port or industrial area are typically:

- **Narrow**: Provincial roads (QL/TL) are often 5–7 meters wide, sometimes less. National highways are 8–12 meters. A blade transport convoy requires at minimum 4.5 meters of clear width, plus escort vehicles.
- **Winding**: Mountain passes in the Central Highlands have hairpin turns with radii of 15–25 meters. A blade on a standard extendable trailer requires a minimum turn radius of 25–35 meters depending on blade length.
- **Structurally limited**: Many rural bridges are designed for 15–30 tonne loads. A nacelle transport weighing 200+ tonnes (component plus trailer) exceeds the capacity of most non-national bridges.
- **Low clearance**: Overhead power lines, traffic signals, pedestrian overpasses, and tree canopies create vertical obstructions. Tower sections at 5.0m diameter plus trailer height can reach 6.0–6.5m total height.

**Real example**: On one project in the Central Highlands, the optimal road route from port to site included 47 identified obstructions — 12 tight corners requiring road widening, 8 bridges requiring structural assessment, 15 overhead cable crossings requiring temporary lifting or rerouting, and 12 locations where roadside structures needed temporary removal or protection.

### Challenge 2: Permits and Approvals

Transporting oversized and overweight cargo in Vietnam requires a cascade of permits and approvals from multiple authorities. This process is time-consuming, politically sensitive, and — critically — starts long before the first truck moves.

**Required permits typically include:**

| Permit Type | Issuing Authority | Typical Duration |
|------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Oversize/overweight transport permit | Provincial Department of Transport | 2–6 weeks |
| Road modification permit | District/Provincial People's Committee | 4–12 weeks |
| Bridge crossing assessment | Transport Engineering Institute | 4–8 weeks |
| Power line lifting/temporary disconnection | Provincial Power Company (EVN subsidiary) | 3–8 weeks |
| Traffic management plan | Provincial Traffic Police | 2–4 weeks |
| Land acquisition (for road widening) | District People's Committee | 3–12 months |
| Environmental impact (for road modifications) | Provincial DONRE | 4–8 weeks |

**The critical path item is almost always land acquisition.** When the route survey identifies corners that require road widening into adjacent land (rice paddies, gardens, private property), the land acquisition process can take 3–12 months depending on:
- Number of affected landowners
- Compensation negotiations
- Local government capacity and political will
- Seasonal agricultural considerations (you can't acquire a rice paddy mid-harvest)

> **From the field:** On one project, we identified a single 90-degree corner in a small village where the road needed widening by 8 meters to accommodate blade passage. The widening required acquisition of approximately 200 square meters from three separate landowners. The negotiation and compensation process took 7 months. That single corner controlled the project's transportation schedule — and therefore the construction start date.

### Challenge 3: Logistics Provider Capability

Wind turbine transportation is a specialized discipline that requires:

- **Specialized vehicle fleets**: Standard trucking companies don't have the right equipment. Blade transport requires extendable or blade-lift trailers. Nacelle and tower transport require multi-axle hydraulic modular trailers (SPMTs) or conventional low-bed trailers with high load capacity.
- **Experienced operators**: Driving a 30-meter trailer carrying a 75-meter blade through a village at night requires exceptional skill. The driver team must coordinate with escort vehicles, manage tail-swing, and navigate obstacles in real-time.
- **Route management capability**: The logistics provider must manage the entire convoy operation — advance route clearing, escort vehicles (front and rear), traffic control, police coordination, and emergency response plans.

In Vietnam, the number of logistics providers with genuine wind turbine transportation experience is limited. During the 2020–2021 rush to meet the FIT deadline, demand for qualified transport providers far exceeded supply. This led to:

- **Price escalation**: Transport costs increased 30–60% over 18 months as demand outstripped capacity
- **Quality compromises**: Some projects engaged inexperienced providers, resulting in cargo damage, road damage, and safety incidents
- **Schedule delays**: Transport providers overcommitted, leading to convoy scheduling conflicts between projects

## Types of Transportation Equipment

### Blade Trailers

| Trailer Type | Description | Blade Length Range | Key Advantage | Key Limitation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Extendable trailer** | Standard trailer with telescoping rear bolster | Up to 65–70m | Lower cost, simpler operation | Limited blade length, poor maneuverability |
| **Blade-lift trailer** | Rear bolster lifts blade tip to 10–30 degrees | Up to 80m+ | Better maneuverability on turns | Higher cost, requires specialized operator |
| **Self-propelled blade transport** | Motorized rear dolly with independent steering | Up to 85m+ | Best maneuverability, shortest swept path | Highest cost, limited availability in Vietnam |

For blades above 65 meters, the blade-lift trailer has become the standard in Vietnam. By tilting the blade tip upward (typically 20–30 degrees), the effective length of the transport unit on the ground is reduced, significantly improving maneuverability at corners and intersections.

### Heavy-Lift Trailers (Nacelle & Tower)

| Equipment | Capacity | Description |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Conventional low-bed trailer** | 60–150 tonnes | Standard heavy haul trailer, 4–8 axle lines |
| **Hydraulic modular trailer (SPMT)** | 100–500+ tonnes | Multi-axle, computer-controlled, each axle independently steered |
| **Multi-axle platform trailer** | 80–200 tonnes | Extended platform with distributed axle loading |

For nacelle transport (100–200 tonnes), SPMTs (Self-Propelled Modular Transporters) are the preferred option in Vietnam when bridge loading is a constraint. SPMTs distribute load across many axles (typically 12–20 axle lines for nacelle transport), reducing the point load on bridge structures and road surfaces.

## Transportation Cost Drivers

Transportation costs for onshore wind in Vietnam vary enormously depending on distance, route complexity, and site accessibility. Typical ranges:

| Cost Component | Range (per turbine set) | Notes |
|---------------|------------------------|-------|
| Port handling & storage | $30–80K | Depends on port capacity, storage duration |
| Route survey & engineering | $50–150K | One-time cost, shared across all turbine sets |
| Road modifications | $100K–$2M+ | Highly variable — can dominate total cost |
| Permits & approvals | $20–80K | Including land acquisition compensation |
| Transport convoy operation | $80–250K | Distance-dependent, typically 50–300 km |
| Escort & traffic management | $15–40K | Police escort, traffic control vehicles |
| Insurance | $30–80K | Cargo + third-party liability |
| **Total per turbine set** | **$300K–$2.5M+** | **Route complexity is the dominant variable** |

For a 100 MW project with 20 turbines, total transportation costs can range from **$6M to $50M+** depending on route complexity. This represents 3–15% of total project CAPEX — a significant cost item that is often underestimated in early-stage project development.

> **Critical insight:** The single biggest variable in transportation cost is road modifications. Projects with sites accessible via national highways and well-maintained provincial roads can achieve transport costs at the lower end. Projects in remote mountain locations requiring extensive road widening, bridge strengthening, and new access road construction can see transport costs that rival foundation costs.

## What Should We Do? The PM's Playbook

### 1. Start the Route Survey Early — Very Early

The transportation route survey should begin in the **feasibility study phase**, not after the EPC contract is signed. Why? Because the route survey results can:
- Influence turbine selection (a smaller turbine with shorter blades may have dramatically lower transport costs)
- Identify fatal flaws that make certain sites uneconomic
- Provide realistic cost inputs for the investment decision
- Begin the permitting and land acquisition process early

**Recommended approach**: Engage a specialized transport route survey company to conduct a desktop study and preliminary site visit during feasibility. Budget $50–100K for this — it's a fraction of the savings from avoiding nasty surprises later.

### 2. Build a Detailed Obstacle Register

Document every obstruction along the route with:
- GPS coordinates
- Photographs (multiple angles)
- Precise dimensions (road width, turn radius, bridge capacity, overhead clearance)
- Proposed mitigation (road widening, temporary removal, alternative route)
- Estimated cost and timeline for mitigation
- Responsible party and permit requirements

This register becomes the master planning document for the entire transport operation. Update it continuously as permits are obtained and modifications are completed.

### 3. Assess Logistics Providers Rigorously

Don't select a transport provider based solely on price. Evaluate:

| Criteria | What to Assess | Red Flags |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Experience** | Number of wind transport projects completed | "We've done heavy transport" (but not blades) |
| **Equipment** | Specific trailer types, crane capacity, SPMT availability | Reliance on rented/subcontracted equipment |
| **Personnel** | Named drivers and supervisors with wind experience | "We'll hire experienced drivers" |
| **Insurance** | Cargo insurance, third-party liability, scope of coverage | Minimum coverage, exclusions for rural roads |
| **Track record** | References from wind developers, damage history | Unwillingness to provide references |
| **Financial capacity** | Balance sheet to support the contract value | Over-leveraged, cash flow dependent on advance payments |

### 4. Plan for Night Operations

In Vietnam, oversized cargo transport is typically restricted to nighttime hours (10 PM–5 AM) on national highways and major provincial roads to minimize traffic disruption. This means:
- Convoy operations take longer than distance alone would suggest
- Driver fatigue management is critical
- Lighting and safety equipment must be robust
- Coordination with traffic police for each night's convoy route must be planned in advance

### 5. Build Weather Contingency into the Schedule

Vietnam's monsoon season (May–November in the central regions) brings heavy rain, flooding, and road deterioration. Mountain passes in the Central Highlands can become impassable during heavy rain events. Budget 2–4 weeks of weather contingency in the transportation schedule, and have contingency storage arrangements at intermediate points along the route.

## Lessons from Things Going Wrong

### Incident: Blade Damage During Transport

On one project, a 67-meter blade sustained tip damage when the extendable trailer's rear bolster caught a roadside utility pole during a nighttime turn. The damage required the blade to be returned to port for repair — a process that took 6 weeks and cost approximately $200K in direct repair costs plus $500K in schedule delay impacts (crane standby at site).

**Root cause**: The obstacle register had identified the utility pole as a "minor obstruction — to be temporarily removed." But the removal permit wasn't obtained before the transport convoy departed. The convoy supervisor decided to proceed, assuming the blade would clear with millimeters of margin. It didn't.

**Lesson**: Never assume margin. If an obstruction is identified, remove it before the convoy starts. The cost of delaying a convoy by one night is a fraction of the cost of blade damage.

### Incident: Bridge Failure

On another project, a 120-tonne nacelle transport crossed a provincial bridge that had been assessed as adequate for the load. During crossing, a bridge deck panel cracked. No cargo was lost, but the bridge was closed for 3 months for repair. The project developer was held liable for repair costs ($300K) and the transport route had to be revised for all subsequent convoys, adding $1.2M to the transport budget.

**Root cause**: The bridge structural assessment was based on as-built drawings from 1995. The actual bridge condition had deteriorated significantly due to 25 years of use and inadequate maintenance. The assessment should have included a physical load test, not just a paper review.

**Lesson**: Always conduct physical bridge assessments (including material testing and load trials where possible) for critical bridge crossings. As-built drawings are a starting point, not a final answer.

## The Honest Take

Wind turbine transportation in Vietnam is one of those topics that sounds straightforward until you're actually managing it. The combination of increasingly large components, rural road networks designed for a different era, complex permitting requirements, and a limited pool of experienced logistics providers creates a risk profile that consistently surprises developers who haven't built in the region before.

The projects that handle transportation well share common characteristics: they start planning early (during feasibility, not after EPC award), they invest in thorough route surveys, they select experienced logistics providers based on capability rather than price alone, and they build realistic schedule and cost contingencies.

The projects that struggle are typically the ones that treat transportation as a logistics line item rather than a critical-path risk management challenge. By the time they discover the 90-degree corner that requires 7 months of land acquisition, it's too late to avoid a schedule delay.

**Start early. Survey thoroughly. Budget honestly. And never try to squeeze a 70-meter blade around a corner with millimeters of clearance at midnight.**
