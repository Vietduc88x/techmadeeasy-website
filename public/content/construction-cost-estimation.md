The moment I realized cost estimation wasn't just math was on a 200 MW onshore wind project where our estimate came in at $280M. The winning bid was $245M. We lost the project — and then watched the winning contractor burn through $310M in actual costs, blow past their schedule by 14 months, and eventually take a $40M write-down.

That project taught me something fundamental: the goal of cost estimation isn't to produce the lowest number. It's to produce the most accurate number. And in renewable energy construction, the gap between an accurate estimate and a hopeful one can destroy a project — or a company.

> Cost estimation is not a math exercise. It's a risk management discipline. The estimate is the first and most consequential decision in any construction project.

According to the Construction Industry Institute (CII), the average cost overrun for large energy infrastructure projects is 28%. For projects where the estimate was rushed or based on inadequate scope definition, overruns regularly exceed 50%. These aren't small numbers — on a $500M offshore wind project, a 28% overrun means $140M of unplanned spending. That's the difference between a profitable investment and a financial disaster.

This article breaks down the construction cost estimation process as it applies to renewable energy projects — the methods, the components, the common mistakes, and the practical lessons from the field.

## Why Cost Estimation Matters More in Renewables

Cost estimation matters in all construction. But it matters disproportionately in renewable energy projects for several reasons:

1. **Tight margins**: Renewable energy projects compete on LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy). A 5% cost overrun can push a project's LCOE above the contracted PPA price, making the entire investment uneconomic.

2. **Non-recourse project finance**: Most large renewable projects are financed through non-recourse project debt. The lenders' security is the project itself — and the financial model that justified the loan. If actual costs exceed the estimate, there's no parent company guarantee to fall back on. The project must absorb the overrun.

3. **Feed-in tariff deadlines**: In markets like Vietnam, projects racing to meet FIT deadlines face enormous schedule pressure. Underestimated costs often lead to scope cuts and quality compromises that create long-term operational problems.

4. **Technology-specific risks**: Each renewable technology has unique cost drivers that general construction estimators often miss — from cable pull-in difficulties in offshore wind to tracker alignment tolerances in utility-scale solar.

## The Three Estimation Methods

### Method 1: Top-Down (Analogous) Estimation

**What it is**: Estimating by comparison with similar completed projects. "Project A was 150 MW onshore wind and cost $210M. Our project is 200 MW, so we scale proportionally."

**Accuracy range**: -30% to +50% (AACE Class 5)

**When to use**: Feasibility studies, early-stage screening, portfolio planning

**Strengths**:
- Fast — can be done in days
- Requires minimal project definition
- Good for "go/no-go" decisions on early-stage opportunities

**Weaknesses**:
- Ignores site-specific conditions
- Doesn't account for market timing (equipment prices, vessel availability)
- Scaling isn't linear (a 200 MW project doesn't cost exactly 1.33x a 150 MW project)

**Practical tip**: Top-down estimates are useful for portfolio screening — deciding which projects are worth developing further. They should never be used for investment decisions, contract pricing, or project financing.

| AACE Estimate Classification | Maturity Level | Accuracy Range | Typical Use |
|------------------------------|---------------|----------------|-------------|
| Class 5 | 0–2% project definition | -30% to +50% | Concept screening |
| Class 4 | 1–15% project definition | -20% to +30% | Feasibility study |
| Class 3 | 10–40% project definition | -15% to +20% | Budget authorization |
| Class 2 | 30–75% project definition | -10% to +15% | Detailed estimate / control |
| Class 1 | 65–100% project definition | -5% to +10% | Tender / final estimate |

Source: AACE International Recommended Practice 18R-97

### Method 2: Parametric Estimation

**What it is**: Using statistical relationships between project parameters and costs. "Onshore wind projects in Vietnam's Central Highlands cost $1.2–1.5M per MW installed, with adjustments for turbine size, hub height, and road access."

**Accuracy range**: -20% to +30% (AACE Class 4)

**When to use**: Pre-feasibility studies, comparative analysis, early budgeting

**Strengths**:
- More accurate than top-down if good parametric data exists
- Enables sensitivity analysis ("What if we use 5 MW turbines instead of 4 MW?")
- Can incorporate regional and technology-specific adjustments

**Weaknesses**:
- Requires a database of comparable projects with reliable cost data
- Parametric relationships may not be linear across all ranges
- Doesn't capture unique site conditions or market timing

**Benchmark cost ranges for renewable energy (2024):**

| Technology | Typical CAPEX Range ($/MW) | Key Cost Drivers |
|-----------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| Onshore wind (Vietnam) | $1.1–1.6M/MW | Turbine size, road access, grid distance |
| Nearshore wind (Vietnam) | $2.0–3.0M/MW | Water depth, foundation type, vessel availability |
| Offshore wind (global) | $3.0–5.5M/MW | Water depth, distance to shore, foundation type |
| Utility-scale solar PV | $0.5–0.9M/MW | Module technology, tracker type, terrain |
| BESS (Li-ion, 4-hour) | $0.8–1.5M/MW | Chemistry, duration, integration scope |

Source: IRENA Renewable Power Generation Costs 2024, BloombergNEF

### Method 3: Bottom-Up (Detailed) Estimation

**What it is**: Building the estimate from individual work activities. Every task in the WBS is quantified — labor hours, equipment rates, material quantities, subcontract packages — and priced individually.

**Accuracy range**: -5% to +15% (AACE Class 1–2)

**When to use**: Tender preparation, EPC contract pricing, project cost control baseline

**Strengths**:
- Most accurate method
- Creates a detailed cost baseline for project control
- Identifies cost drivers at the activity level
- Enables value engineering and scope optimization

**Weaknesses**:
- Time-intensive (weeks to months for a large project)
- Requires detailed design and scope definition
- Quality depends entirely on the estimator's experience and the accuracy of input data

**Practical tip**: A bottom-up estimate is only as good as the quantity take-off that feeds it. If the quantities are wrong, the estimate is wrong — no matter how sophisticated the pricing model. Always verify quantities against design drawings independently.

## Anatomy of a Construction Cost Estimate

### Direct Costs

Direct costs are the costs that can be attributed to specific construction activities. They typically represent 65–80% of total project cost.

| Direct Cost Category | Description | Typical % of Direct Costs |
|---------------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Equipment / Materials** | Turbines, panels, cables, transformers, structural steel, concrete | 55–70% |
| **Labor** | Site workers, crane operators, electricians, welders, supervisors | 10–20% |
| **Plant & Equipment** | Crane hire, vessel charter, earthmoving equipment, temporary works | 10–20% |
| **Subcontractors** | Specialist packages — piling, cable jointing, HV testing, civil works | 5–15% |

**For a typical 200 MW onshore wind project in Vietnam:**

| Item | Estimated Cost | % of Total |
|------|---------------|-----------|
| Wind turbines (TSA) | $150–180M | 55–60% |
| Civil works & foundations | $20–30M | 8–10% |
| Electrical BOP (cables, substation) | $15–25M | 6–8% |
| Transportation & logistics | $8–15M | 3–5% |
| Installation & erection | $10–15M | 4–5% |
| **Total direct costs** | **$203–265M** | **~75%** |

### Indirect Costs

Indirect costs support the construction activities but aren't tied to specific tasks. They typically represent 15–25% of total project cost.

| Indirect Cost Category | Description | Duration Dependency |
|-----------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Site establishment** | Offices, workshops, storage, access roads, utilities | Non-recurring (one-time) |
| **Project management staff** | PM, construction manager, QA/QC, HSE, admin | Recurring (monthly) |
| **Insurance** | CAR (Contractor's All Risk), third-party liability, marine transit | Recurring |
| **Permits & approvals** | Construction permits, environmental compliance, grid connection | Non-recurring |
| **Mobilization / demobilization** | Equipment transport to/from site, crane assembly | Non-recurring |
| **Temporary works** | Scaffolding, formwork, temporary power, dewatering | Project-specific |

> **Critical distinction — recurring vs. non-recurring**: This matters because schedule delays disproportionately inflate recurring indirect costs. If your project runs 3 months late, every monthly salary, equipment rental, and insurance premium for those 3 months is pure cost overrun. This is why schedule accuracy is inseparable from cost accuracy.

### Risk and Contingency

Every responsible estimate includes a contingency provision — a budgeted amount for risks that are identified but not yet quantifiable at the individual activity level.

**How to size contingency:**

| Project Phase | Typical Contingency Range | Justification |
|--------------|--------------------------|---------------|
| Feasibility (Class 5) | 20–30% of base estimate | High uncertainty, limited scope definition |
| Pre-FEED (Class 4) | 15–20% | Better scope, still significant unknowns |
| FEED (Class 3) | 10–15% | Detailed design available, key contracts identified |
| Detailed design (Class 2) | 5–10% | Most scope defined, major contracts awarded |
| Construction (Class 1) | 3–5% | Execution contingency only |

**Common mistake**: Setting contingency as a flat percentage without linking it to identified risks. A proper contingency should be derived from a risk register — each identified risk is assessed for probability and impact, and the aggregate expected value informs the contingency amount. This is often called a "risk-based contingency" or "probabilistic contingency" approach.

For larger projects ($200M+), Monte Carlo simulation is the gold standard. You model each cost line item as a probability distribution (not a single number), run thousands of simulations, and derive the contingency needed to achieve a target confidence level (typically P75 or P80).

### Corporate Overheads

These are the general and administrative costs of running the contracting or development company — head office rent, corporate staff, IT systems, legal, finance, HR. They're typically applied as a percentage of total project cost (3–8% depending on company size and project value).

### Profit / Fee

The contractor's or developer's margin. For EPC contracts in renewable energy, typical profit margins range from 5–12% depending on competition intensity, risk profile, and strategic importance of the project.

## The Estimation Process: Step by Step

### Step 1: Scope Definition and Understanding

Before estimating anything, understand what you're estimating:
- Read every tender document, specification, and drawing
- Identify scope gaps and ambiguities — these are where cost surprises hide
- Clarify interfaces with other packages and contractors
- Confirm design stage and maturity of engineering deliverables

### Step 2: Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

Decompose the project into manageable, estimable activities. A well-structured WBS is the backbone of a reliable estimate. For a wind farm, this typically follows:

- Level 1: Project
- Level 2: Work packages (TSA, BOP, Civil, Electrical, T&I)
- Level 3: Disciplines (foundations, roads, cabling, substations)
- Level 4: Activities (excavation, concrete pouring, cable pulling, testing)
- Level 5: Tasks (individual measurable work items)

### Step 3: Quantity Take-Off

For each activity, determine the physical quantities:
- Cubic meters of concrete
- Tonnes of structural steel
- Linear meters of cable
- Number of pile driving cycles
- Hours of crane time

### Step 4: Rate Application

Apply unit rates to each quantity:
- Labor rates ($/hour by skill level)
- Material prices (from supplier quotes or database)
- Equipment rates ($/day or $/hour by equipment type)
- Subcontract rates (from market inquiries or historical data)

### Step 5: Indirect Cost Build-Up

Develop the indirect cost budget:
- Staff loading chart (who is on site, for how long)
- Site establishment scope and cost
- Insurance and permit costs
- Mobilization and demobilization costs

### Step 6: Risk Assessment and Contingency

Develop the risk register, assess probability and impact, and derive the contingency provision.

### Step 7: Review and Validation

The most important step — and the most frequently skipped:
- **Internal peer review**: Another experienced estimator reviews the estimate independently
- **Benchmarking**: Compare the estimate against comparable projects on a $/MW or $/unit basis
- **Sensitivity analysis**: Test which assumptions drive the most cost variance
- **Management review**: Present the estimate to leadership with clear documentation of assumptions, exclusions, and risks

## Common Estimation Mistakes in Renewable Energy

### Mistake 1: Underestimating Mobilization Costs

In offshore and nearshore wind, equipment mobilization (crane barges, installation vessels, heavy-lift equipment) can represent 50–60% of total BOP costs. Estimators who haven't worked in marine construction regularly underestimate vessel day rates, fuel consumption, weather standby, and the cost of customs clearance for imported equipment.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Schedule Risk

An estimate without a linked schedule is incomplete. If the schedule slips, recurring indirect costs escalate immediately. A common trap: the base estimate assumes a 12-month construction period, but the schedule analysis shows 15 months is more realistic. Those 3 extra months of site management, equipment rental, and insurance can add 10–15% to total cost.

### Mistake 3: Using Stale Pricing Data

Material and equipment prices in renewable energy are volatile. Steel prices fluctuated 40% between 2021 and 2023. Turbine prices have increased 20–30% from their 2020 lows. Using historical pricing without adjustment for current market conditions is a recipe for underestimation.

### Mistake 4: Flat-Rate Contingency

Applying a blanket 10% contingency without a risk register is lazy and often wrong. Some projects need 5% contingency (well-defined scope, proven technology, experienced team). Others need 20% (new market, unproven technology, complex permitting). The contingency should reflect the actual risk profile, not a thumb-in-the-air percentage.

### Mistake 5: Scope Gaps at Interfaces

In multi-package projects, the gaps between contractor scopes are where costs hide. Who pays for the cable pull-in — the cable supplier or the installation contractor? Who provides the lifting attachments — the foundation fabricator or the T&I contractor? These interface costs are small individually but can aggregate to millions on a large project.

## Cash Flow Analysis: The Forgotten Step

A cost estimate tells you how much the project will cost in total. A cash flow analysis tells you when you need the money. This distinction is critical because:

- **Financing costs**: Money borrowed earlier costs more in interest. An accurate cash flow forecast optimizes the drawdown schedule and minimizes financing costs.
- **Working capital**: Contractors need to fund costs before receiving payment milestones. A cash-negative period can create liquidity crises even on profitable projects.
- **Milestone payments**: EPC contracts are typically paid against milestone achievements. The estimate must align with the payment schedule to ensure cost tracking is meaningful.

## The Honest Take

Cost estimation in renewable energy construction is simultaneously an art and a science. The science is in the quantities, the rates, the databases, and the statistical methods. The art is in the judgment — knowing which risks to quantify, which assumptions to challenge, and which historical comparisons are relevant.

The estimators who produce the most reliable numbers share common traits: they've been on construction sites, they understand what goes wrong and why, they're skeptical of optimistic assumptions, and they're willing to defend an honest number against commercial pressure to reduce it.

The most expensive estimate is not the one with the highest number. It's the one with the lowest number that turns out to be wrong. An underestimate that wins a contract but leads to cost overruns, schedule delays, and quality compromises costs far more — in money, reputation, and stress — than an honest estimate that correctly prices the risk.

**Build the estimate you'd bet your own money on. If you wouldn't invest your personal savings based on these numbers, don't ask your client or lender to either.**
